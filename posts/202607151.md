*I can read docs, write code, and ship in English all day. But in meetings I freeze. Here's the study plan I put together to fix it — sharing it in case you're in the same spot.*
 
---
 
I'll be honest about my starting point, because I think a lot of devs quietly live here too.
 
I can read any documentation. I write commits, PR descriptions, and long Slack threads in English without thinking about it. But the moment someone on a call says *"so, what's your update?"*, something breaks. I know exactly what I'd say in Spanish — and out comes something flat, slow, three levels below how I actually think.
 
I got tired of that gap, so instead of "studying English" vaguely, I sat down and built myself an actual plan. This post is that plan. I'm not writing it as someone who nailed it — I'm in the middle of it. But organizing it this way already made it click, so I figured I'd share.
 
## The reframe that changed how I practice
 
The first thing I had to admit: this isn't a knowledge problem. If I can read and write English, my grammar and vocabulary aren't the bottleneck. I already have the pieces.
 
What I don't have is **automatism** — the phrases loaded and ready, so I'm not translating in my head in real time while five people wait for me to finish a sentence.
 
That reframe killed a bunch of options I was tempted by:
 
- Grammar courses fix a problem I don't have.
- Duolingo would keep me at beginner level forever.
- Passively watching series builds recognition, not the thing I actually need: *production*, under time pressure.
A meeting is a production skill on a clock. So I decided to train it the way you'd train anything physical — reps, out loud, on repeat.
 
## What I noticed about meetings
 
Once I paid attention, I realized a work meeting isn't open-ended. The same handful of *functions* come up every single time. Giving an update. Asking for clarification. Buying time while I think. Agreeing. Pushing back without sounding rude. Proposing something. Wrapping up.
 
So my bet is: there are maybe 30–40 fixed phrases that cover most of what I'll ever need to say in a meeting. If I get those automatic, I stop translating and start talking.
 
The two categories I decided to attack first surprised me — they're not fancy vocabulary:
 
1. **Asking for clarification.** Repeating back what I understood in my own words buys time *and* prevents misunderstandings.
2. **Buying time.** Having a clean way to say "let me think for a second" is the difference between a confident pause and a panic freeze.
My theory is that once those two are solid, half the anxiety goes away — because I'm no longer scared of the moment I don't understand or don't have the answer ready.
 
## The plan I'm actually running
 
Four rules I set for myself:
 
**1. Organize the phrases by function, not by grammar.** By what I'm trying to *do* in the moment — updating, clarifying, disagreeing, closing — so I reach for them the way they come up.
 
**2. Practice out loud, never in silence.** This is the one I keep reminding myself, because it's the whole game. The pattern gets built in the mouth, not the eyes. Reading a list silently does almost nothing.
 
**3. Ten minutes of shadowing a day.** Put on a recorded standup or tech talk and repeat *over* the speaker, copying the rhythm — not translating. Native speakers glue words together (*wanna, gonna, kinda*) and rush the small words. This fixes the "flow" without learning a single new word.
 
**4. Use it in a real meeting that same week.** Passive knowledge doesn't show up live. Only what I've already used becomes a reflex.
 
## The syllabus I built
 
I broke it into one-hour blocks. I take one, drill it out loud until it's automatic, and only then move on:
 
1. **Daily / standup** — updates, blockers, estimates, asking for help
2. **Asking for clarification** — when I didn't catch it
3. **Buying time** — not freezing
4. **Giving my opinion** — taking a position
5. **Agreeing / disagreeing** — pushing back without sounding rude
6. **Proposing** — floating ideas without imposing them
7. **Interrupting** — taking the floor politely
8. **Closing** — summarizing and defining next steps
9. **Code review** — feedback, edge cases, asking *why*
10. **Planning / estimation** — dependencies, uncertainty
11. **Explaining a bug** — root cause, quick fix vs. real fix
12. **1:1 with my manager** — bringing topics, asking for feedback
13. **Technical interviews** — walking through my approach, trade-offs, reasoning out loud
Plus ten minutes of shadowing at the start of every block.
 
They group into three families: **managing the conversation** (2, 3, 5, 7), **contributing content** (1, 4, 6, 8), and **technical scenarios** (9–13). I'm starting with 1, 2, and 3 — my guess is that's the fastest path out of feeling basic.
 
## Where I'm at
 
That's the plan. I'll report back on whether it actually works once I've run it for a few weeks.
 
If you're a dev who codes in English fine but freezes the second a call starts — I don't think it's your English. I think it's the reps. That's the bet I'm making on myself, anyway.
