## 1:1 With My Manager — Bringing Topics, Asking for Feedback

## Introduction

Step 12 of the plan from *"How I'm training myself to stop sounding basic in english meetings"*.

Thirty minutes, two people, no agenda. It should be the easiest meeting of the week — small, low stakes, nobody watching. It's the one I waste the most.

---

## Why This Matters

My default 1:1 goes like this: they ask questions, I answer them, we finish early, everyone says "good chat". I brought nothing. I left with nothing.

Two things cause that.

The first is language. A 1:1 is open-ended — there's no ticket to describe, no status to give. Small talk and career conversations need a wider range of English than "the endpoint is done", and I don't have it loaded.

The second isn't language at all. In an open meeting with no structure, whoever prepared runs it. I never prepared, so I never ran it.

The good news: this is the one meeting where **preparing beforehand completely solves the English problem**. Three bullet points written in advance and I'm not improvising anymore.

---

## The Core Structure

Write three things before the call. That's the whole method.

```
1. Something I need (decision, access, help, a blocker)
2. Something about how I'm working (feedback, growth, friction)
3. Something about where I'm going (scope, direction, next project)
```

Bring one from each and you never have a dead 1:1 again. Say them at the start:

```
I've got three things on my list — the staging access, some
feedback on the last project, and I want to talk about what I
pick up next. Anything on your side first?
```

That one sentence turns it from an interview into a meeting with an agenda.

## Situations & Examples

### Situation 1: They Open With "How's It Going?"

**Context:** The default opener. My default answer wasted it every time.

**Bad way:**
```
Good, yeah. Busy. The API stuff is going fine.
```
❌ Polite, empty, and now they have to think of the next question.

**Good way:**
```
Going well — the integration shipped Tuesday. I do have a few
things I want to cover today: staging access, and I'd like
your read on how the last project went.
```
✅ Short answer, then take the wheel.

---

### Situation 2: Asking for Feedback

**Context:** The thing I most want and most avoid asking for, because "how am I doing?" is a terrifying question to ask in a second language.

**Bad way:**
```
So... how am I doing?
```
❌ Too broad. You get "yeah, good, no complaints", which tells you nothing.

**Good way:**
```
I'd like some feedback on the payments project specifically.
If you had to pick one thing I should have done differently
there — what would it be?
```
✅ Narrow question, and "pick one thing" makes it easy to answer honestly.

**Ask about one project, not about yourself.** Specific questions get specific answers.

---

### Situation 3: Receiving Feedback You Didn't Expect

**Context:** They say something critical. In English, under mild stress, my instinct is to explain myself immediately.

**Bad way:**
```
Yeah but that was because the requirements changed and I
didn't have access to...
```
❌ Now they'll think twice before giving you feedback again.

**Good way:**
```
Okay, that's useful — let me make sure I understand. You mean
I should have flagged the delay earlier, rather than trying to
catch up quietly? [pause] Yeah, that's fair. I'll do that.
```
✅ Repeat it back (**Lesson 2**), take the pause (**Lesson 3**), then respond.

If you genuinely disagree, say so *after* you've understood it, not instead of.

---

### Situation 4: Raising Something That Bothers You

**Context:** Something is wrong — too many meetings, unclear priorities, a process that doesn't work. This is what 1:1s are for and it's the hardest thing to say.

**Bad way:**
```
[Never mention it. Get frustrated for six months.]
```
❌ They can't fix what they don't know about.

**Good way:**
```
There's something I want to raise, and it's not a complaint —
just something that's slowing me down. I'm in about four hours
of meetings a day this sprint, so the actual coding happens
after 6pm. Is there anything we can drop?
```
✅ "Not a complaint, just something slowing me down" is the phrase that makes this safe to say.

Bring a fact, not a mood. "Four hours a day" is discussable. "I'm overwhelmed" is harder to act on.

---

### Situation 5: Talking About What You Want Next

**Context:** More responsibility, a different area, a specific project. Nobody offers this to people who never mention it.

**Bad way:**
```
[Wait to be offered something]
```
❌ Your manager isn't guessing what you want.

**Good way:**
```
Something I've been thinking about: I'd like to get more into
the architecture side rather than only feature work. Is that
realistic in the next few months, and what would I need to
show to get there?
```
✅ Names the direction, asks what the path looks like. Concrete for both of you.

---

### Situation 6: Giving Feedback Upwards

**Context:** They asked "anything I could do differently?" and you have an actual answer.

**Bad way:**
```
No, no, everything's great.
```
❌ Wasted the one moment they explicitly opened the door.

**Good way:**
```
One thing that would help me: when priorities change
mid-sprint, if I could hear the reason as well as the change,
it's much easier for me to make the right trade-offs on my own.
```
✅ Specific, framed as what helps you, not what they're doing wrong.

---

### Situation 7: There's Genuinely Nothing to Discuss

**Context:** Quiet week. It happens.

**Good way:**
```
Nothing urgent from my side this week. If you're happy, we
can keep this short — or I'm curious how the roadmap
conversation went, if you can share.
```
✅ Honest, and offers something rather than sitting in silence.

## Phrases

### Taking the wheel at the start

```
I've got a few things on my list today.
Three things from my side, then I'm all yours.
Before we start — is there anything you need from me?
Can I go through my list first?
```

### Asking for feedback

```
I'd like some feedback on the X project specifically.
If you had to pick one thing I should improve, what would it be?
What's one thing I could have done better there?
How did that land from your side?
Is there anything you'd want me to do differently next time?
Am I focusing on the right things right now?
```

### Receiving feedback

```
That's useful, thanks.
Let me make sure I understand — you mean...?
Can you give me an example of when that happened?
That's fair, I'll work on it.
I hadn't seen it that way. Let me think about it.
```

### Raising a problem

```
There's something I want to raise — it's not a complaint.
Something that's slowing me down at the moment: ...
I want to flag something before it becomes a bigger issue.
Can I be honest about something?
Here's the friction I'm running into: ...
```

### Talking about direction

```
Something I've been thinking about: ...
I'd like to get more involved in X.
Where do you see me going in the next six months?
What would I need to show to move towards X?
Is that realistic here, or is it the wrong place for it?
```

### Giving feedback upwards

```
One thing that would help me: ...
It's easier for me when...
Could we try X for a couple of sprints and see?
Small thing, but it makes a difference: ...
```

### Closing

```
That's everything from my side.
So the next steps are: you check X, I'll do Y.
Can we come back to this next time?
Same time next week?
```

## Key Rules

1. **Write three bullets before the call**
   - This single habit fixes both the English problem and the empty-meeting problem.
2. **Answer "how's it going" in one sentence, then take the wheel**
   - Don't let it become a question-and-answer session.
3. **Ask for feedback on one project, not on yourself**
   - Broad questions get "yeah, good".
4. **"Pick one thing" gets you an honest answer**
   - It gives them permission to say something real.
5. **Never defend against feedback in the moment**
   - Understand it, then respond. Explaining first shuts the door.
6. **Bring a fact, not a mood**
   - "Four hours of meetings a day" beats "I'm overwhelmed".
7. **Say what you want out loud, at least once a quarter**
   - Nobody is going to guess it.
8. **Take notes, and check them next time**
   - "Last time you said X — I've been trying Y" is worth a lot.

## What NOT to Do

| ❌ Don't say | ✅ Say instead |
|---|---|
| "Good, busy, all fine." | "Going well. I've got three things on my list." |
| "How am I doing?" | "One thing I could have done better on the X project?" |
| "Yeah but that was because..." | "Let me make sure I understand — you mean...?" |
| "Everything's great." (when asked for feedback) | "One thing that would help me: ..." |
| *Never mentioning the problem* | "Not a complaint, but here's what's slowing me down." |
| *Waiting to be offered growth* | "I'd like to move towards X — what would that take?" |
| *Turning up with nothing* | *Three bullets, written the night before.* |

## The Bigger Picture

The 1:1 is the only meeting that's entirely about me, and for a long time I let someone else drive it. That's not a language problem — it's what happens when you arrive unprepared to an open-ended conversation in a second language, so you default to reacting.

Preparation is the cheat code here. Three bullets on a sticky note and the English stops mattering, because I'm not improvising anymore. I already know what I want to say. I just have to say it.
