## Interrupting — Taking the Floor Politely

## Introduction

Step 7 of the plan from *"How I'm training myself to stop sounding basic in english meetings"*.

The previous blocks assume someone hands me the floor. This one is about the meetings where nobody does — four people talking, no pauses, and everything I wanted to say arrives ten minutes too late.

---

## Why This Matters

This is the block I need most, and it's not really an English problem.

In a call with native speakers there is no gap. They don't pause between sentences the way I need them to. So I wait for a natural opening that never comes, the topic moves on, and I say nothing for forty minutes.

Then afterwards I write the comment in Slack, where it changes nothing.

The uncomfortable truth: **native speakers interrupt each other constantly.** It's not rude there — it's how the conversation works. I was being polite by a rule nobody else in the meeting was following.

---

## The Core Structure

```
[Signal] + [Reason] + [Point]

Sorry, quick one before we move on — that endpoint is already
deprecated, so we'd be building on something that goes away
in October.
```

- **Signal** — a short sound that claims the turn. "Sorry —", "Quick one —", "Can I jump in?"
- **Reason** — why it can't wait. This is what makes the interruption acceptable.
- **Point** — get to it fast. You took the floor by force; be quick with it.

Say the signal *loudly and immediately*. Starting quietly and hoping to be noticed doesn't work — that's the mistake I keep making.

---

## Situations & Examples

### Situation 1: Nobody Is Pausing

**Context:** The classic. Everyone is talking and there's no gap.

**Bad way:**
```
[Wait for an opening. It never comes. Say nothing.]
```
❌ Forty minutes of silence, then a Slack message nobody acts on.

**Good way:**
```
Sorry — can I jump in for a second?
[wait one beat, then go]
```
✅ "Sorry, can I jump in" is a complete tool. It works nearly every time.

**On a video call**, unmuting and saying "sorry —" is itself the signal. People stop.

---

### Situation 2: They're About to Decide Something Wrong

**Context:** The one interruption nobody minds. Waiting costs the team real money.

**Bad way:**
```
[Wait until the end, then: "actually, about that earlier
thing..."]
```
❌ Too late. The decision is made and now reopening it is expensive.

**Good way:**
```
Sorry to cut in — before we lock this in, that table has about
40 million rows. A full scan there takes minutes, not seconds.
```
✅ "Before we lock this in" makes the urgency the reason for the interruption.

---

### Situation 3: You Got Lost Three Sentences Ago

**Context:** You stopped understanding a while back and it's getting worse.

**Bad way:**
```
[Keep nodding, understand nothing for the rest of the meeting]
```
❌ Every minute after that point is wasted.

**Good way:**
```
Sorry, can I stop you for one second? When you say "the
consumer" — do you mean the queue consumer or the client app?
```
✅ Interrupt early, when it's one question. Wait, and it becomes ten.

---

### Situation 4: Someone Interrupted *You*

**Context:** You were mid-sentence and got cut off. Happens to me a lot, and I used to just let the point go.

**Bad way:**
```
[Let it go, never finish the sentence]
```
❌ It happens twice, and the room learns your turns are interruptible.

**Good way:**
```
Let me just finish that thought — the part I hadn't got to is
that the cache expires nightly, so this only breaks after
midnight.
```
✅ Calm, no complaint about the interruption, just take the turn back.

---

### Situation 5: The Meeting Has Gone Off Track

**Context:** Ten minutes on something unrelated and you can see the agenda burning.

**Bad way:**
```
[Watch the clock, say nothing, run out of time]
```
❌ Everyone in the room wanted someone to do this.

**Good way:**
```
Sorry to interrupt — I want to make sure we get to the release
plan. Should we take this one offline?
```
✅ Interrupting to protect the meeting reads as leadership, not rudeness.

---

### Situation 6: You Only Need Two Seconds

**Context:** A number, a link, a correction. You don't need the floor, just a moment.

**Good way:**
```
Quick one: it's 2.4, not 2.1.
Small correction — that shipped last week.
Just to flag: that's on the other team's roadmap.
```
✅ Announce it's short and people give you the room without resistance.

## Phrases

### Taking the floor

```
Sorry, can I jump in?
Can I add something quickly?
Sorry to interrupt —
Quick one before we move on...
Can I stop you for a second?
Mind if I jump in here?
```

### Making the urgency the reason

```
Before we lock this in...
Sorry, this is relevant right now...
While we're on this topic...
I want to catch this before we move on...
```

### Signalling it's short

```
Quick one:
Small thing:
Just to flag:
Thirty seconds and I'm done:
One quick correction —
```

### Taking your turn back

```
Let me just finish that thought.
Sorry, I hadn't finished — the part I wanted to add is...
Going back to what I was saying...
Can I complete that point?
```

### Redirecting the meeting

```
Sorry to interrupt — should we take this offline?
I want to make sure we get to X before we run out of time.
Are we still on the main topic here?
Can we park this and come back to it?
```

### Handing it back after

```
Sorry, go ahead.
That's all from me.
Sorry, you were saying?
Okay, back to you.
```

## Key Rules

1. **Say the signal loudly and immediately**
   - A quiet "um..." is not an interruption, it's noise.
2. **Give a reason within one sentence**
   - "Sorry — before we lock this in" is accepted. A bare "sorry" is not.
3. **Be short**
   - You took the turn by force. Thirty seconds, then give it back.
4. **Hand the floor back explicitly**
   - "Sorry, go ahead" turns an interruption into a normal exchange.
5. **Interrupt early when you're lost**
   - One question now beats ten questions later.
6. **On video, unmuting is the signal**
   - Don't wait for a gap that video-call latency guarantees won't come.
7. **You're not being rude**
   - Everyone else in that meeting is doing it. Politeness that leaves you silent for an hour isn't politeness.

---

## What NOT to Do

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---|---|
| Wait for a gap that never comes | "Sorry, can I jump in?" |
| Start quietly and hope to be heard | Say the first word clearly and loud |
| Interrupt and then talk for four minutes | Interrupt, make one point, hand it back |
| Nod while understanding nothing | "Sorry, can I stop you for a second?" |
| Let someone cut you off permanently | "Let me just finish that thought." |
| Save it for Slack afterwards | Say it while the decision is open |
| Apologize three times before the point | One "sorry", then the point |

## The Bigger Picture

Interrupting isn't a language skill, it's a permission problem. I had to give myself permission first, and the phrases only made it easier once I had.

The version of me who stays quiet for the whole call isn't being respectful. He's just absent — and from the outside those two look identical.
