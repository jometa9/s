Right now I train 5 to 7 times per week. That's easy, because I set my own schedule and my job has no fixed hours.

But I'm looking for a new job, a 9 to 5. With those hours I can't go train at 15:00 when the gym is empty. Only at 7 am, before work, every day, forever.

So the real question is not "can I keep training 6 times a week". The question is what happens to me when I can't.

Because I don't train for health. I train for identity.

> "I train every day" = "I'm a consistent and disciplined person"

That's the deal I made with myself years ago. And the problem with that deal is that it's welded to a number. Six sessions a week means I'm consistent. Four means I'm slipping. Two means I'm someone else.

That's a fragile place to build an identity. One schedule change away from collapse.

The way out was obvious once I saw it: training was never the only proof. I code every day. I write and study every day. Consistency isn't something I do at the gym, it's something I am, and the gym is just one of the places it shows up. Attaching all of it to a single activity was stupid.

So I can lower the floor without losing myself.

Counting only the gym I have more than 8 years, and doing sports in general, easily more than 15 (I'm 28). Tennis, rugby, skate and longboard, running, weights. At the end of the day my favorite is training muscle at the gym: a place where you don't depend on the weather, just a shed with metal structures and weight plates.

I'm not walking away from any of that. I'm building a version of it that no job can take from me. Three days a week, simple enough to survive any schedule, hard enough to still grow. I'm looking at this as a lifetime structure, not a phase.

## The Basics

- Three times per week
- Focus on intensity, hard sets
- Consistency over time
- High protein diet

## The Plan

Let's try it for 6 months (yes, six months, not one).
