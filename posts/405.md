I write a post almost every day. My English is improving: better words, fewer grammar mistakes, faster typing without translating in my head first.

But yesterday I reread my post about training, and something was wrong.

Not the grammar. I checked every sentence and most of them were fine. The problem was that the sentences were correct and the post was not. Each paragraph made sense alone, and together they made nothing. Three separate essays stacked on top of each other, and the reader has to restart twice.

So here's the question I couldn't answer: **why do correct sentences make a broken post?**

## The comfortable explanation

My first answer was "my English is not good enough yet."

That's a comfortable explanation, because the solution is easy. Study more vocabulary. Learn more phrasal verbs. Do more reps. Keep doing exactly what I'm already doing, just more.

The problem is that it's wrong. And if I believe it, I study the wrong thing for two years and my posts stay broken while my vocabulary gets excellent.

Here's how I know it's wrong: I write the same way in Spanish. Same jumps, same stacked topics, same missing connections. My native language doesn't save me. So it was never a language problem.

## What was actually missing

I was writing **sections**, not a **story**.

A section is a topic with a title. You can write ten good sections and still have nothing, because the reader has no reason to move from one to the next. A story is different: each part exists because of the part before it.

And in my training post, the specific thing missing was the **question**. I never wrote it. I had the situation, I had my feelings about it, I had the answer — but I never stopped to say, in one sentence, what I was actually asking.

Without that sentence the reader has no thread. Every section starts from zero.

## The structure I'm using now

Seven beats. In order.

1. **The Situation** — where I am now. A fact, a number, a place. Not an idea.
2. **The Break** — what changed, or is about to change. If nothing breaks, there's no post.
3. **The Question** — one explicit sentence. The beat I always skip.
4. **The Stakes** — why it matters to me, specifically.
5. **The Turn** — the one thing I realized. One. If I have two, the second one is another post.
6. **The Answer** — what I'm doing about it. Lists are allowed here.
7. **The Bet** — a date or a metric. How I'll know if it worked.

This post is written with those seven beats. You just read beats 1 to 5 without noticing.

## The English part: two tricks

Now the part that *is* about language.

Connecting paragraphs in English happens almost entirely in **the first sentence of the paragraph**. Two techniques, both mechanical enough that I can apply them while editing:

**The echo.** Repeat a word from the end of one paragraph at the start of the next one.

> ...only at 7 am, before work, every day, forever.
>
> So the real **question** is not "can I keep training 6 times a week". The **question** is what happens when I can't.

That repeated word is a handrail. The reader can't fall in the gap between paragraphs.

**The bridge phrase.** Open the paragraph with a phrase that announces what kind of move is coming:

- Cause: *So... / That's why... / Which means...*
- Contrast: *But... / The problem is... / Except that...*
- Escalation: *And it gets worse. / Not just X — Y.*
- Realization: *Then I realized... / It's not X. It's Y.*
- Landing: *So here's what I'm doing. / That's the whole idea.*

One per paragraph. Never two.

I used to think native writers had hundreds of these. They don't. They reuse the same ten, constantly. Variety was never the goal.

## The test

This is the part I actually needed, more than the whole structure.

Read **only** the last sentence of each paragraph plus the first sentence of the next one. Skip everything in between.

If each pair makes sense alone, the dots connect. If you feel a jump, that's the exact location of the problem — and it's usually one missing sentence, not a rewrite.

The test works because it turns a vague feeling ("something is off, I don't know where") into a line number. For someone learning the language, that's the difference between fixing a post and rewriting it five times hoping it improves.

## The bet

From now on, every post here follows the seven beats and passes the gap test before I publish it.

Two things I'm watching:

- **Beat 3.** I skip it. Every time. If a reader can't repeat my question back in their own words, I failed before I wrote a single paragraph.
- **The rewrite count.** Right now I rewrite a post three or four times and it still feels disconnected. If the structure works, that number goes down.

In three months I read these posts again and we see if it worked.
