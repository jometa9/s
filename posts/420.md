## Planning / Estimation — Dependencies and Uncertainty

## Introduction

Step 10 of the plan from *"How I'm training myself to stop sounding basic in english meetings"*.

This is the block where bad English costs actual money. If I say "three days" when I mean "three days if nothing else comes up and if the API team answers me on Monday", someone puts three days in a spreadsheet and a client hears a date.

---

## Why This Matters

Estimating is a conversation about **uncertainty**, and uncertainty is exactly the thing my English flattens.

In Spanish I can say something is roughly a week, probably more like two if the integration is messy, and I have no idea until I see the API docs. Three different levels of confidence in one sentence, without thinking.

In English that whole range collapses into one number. And a number with no confidence attached gets treated as a promise.

The fix isn't hedging everything. It's being able to say *how sure I am* as clearly as *what the number is*.

---

## The Core Structure

```
[Estimate] + [Confidence] + [Assumption]

Three to four days for the endpoint. I'm fairly confident on
that part — it assumes the auth service already returns the
role, which I haven't verified yet.
```

- **Estimate** — a range, not a point. Ranges are honest and nobody argues with them.
- **Confidence** — "fairly confident", "rough guess", "I'd bet on this".
- **Assumption** — the thing that, if wrong, blows up the number. Say it out loud so it's shared.

The assumption is the part I used to leave in my head. It's the most important sentence of the three.

## Situations & Examples

### Situation 1: You're Asked for an Estimate on the Spot

**Context:** "How long will this take?" — with no time to think.

**Bad way:**
```
Um... maybe three days?
```
❌ A number you invented under pressure, now treated as a commitment.

**Good way:**
```
Rough guess, and I'd like to check before you plan around it:
somewhere between three days and a week. The spread is because
I don't know yet if we need a migration. Can I confirm by
tomorrow morning?
```
✅ Gives them something now, tells them how much to trust it, and sets a time to be exact.

This is **Lesson 3 (buying time)** applied to numbers.

---

### Situation 2: You Genuinely Don't Know

**Context:** You've never touched this part of the system. Any number would be fiction.

**Bad way:**
```
I don't know.
```
❌ True, but it stops the planning conversation dead.

**Good way:**
```
I can't estimate this honestly yet — I've never worked in that
service. Could I spend half a day looking at it and come back
with a real number this afternoon?
```
✅ "I need a spike before I can estimate" is a professional answer, not an excuse.

---

### Situation 3: You Depend on Someone Else

**Context:** Your part is small. The blocker is another team, a review, or an approval.

**Bad way:**
```
Two days.
```
❌ Two days of *your* work. The calendar time might be two weeks.

**Good way:**
```
My part is about two days of work. But it's blocked until the
data team gives us the new schema, and I don't have a date for
that. So two days of work, unknown calendar time.
```
✅ Separating **work time** from **calendar time** is the most useful distinction in this whole block.

---

### Situation 4: The Estimate Is Too Optimistic

**Context:** Someone says "this is a two-day thing, right?" and it is not.

**Bad way:**
```
Yeah, should be fine.
```
❌ You just agreed to a deadline you'll miss.

**Good way:**
```
I'd push back on that one. Two days is the happy path — but
this touches the payment flow, so testing alone is a day. I'd
say four to five to do it properly.
```
✅ Say the number out loud with the reason. Agreeing now and missing later is much worse.

---

### Situation 5: You're Going to Miss the Estimate

**Context:** You're halfway through and it's clearly taking longer.

**Bad way:**
```
[Say nothing, hope to catch up, tell them on the deadline]
```
❌ The cost isn't the delay, it's the surprise.

**Good way:**
```
Update on the integration: I'm going to need two more days.
The webhooks are less documented than I expected and I spent
most of yesterday reverse-engineering the payload. I'd rather
tell you now than on Friday.
```
✅ Early, with the reason, without apologizing five times.

---

### Situation 6: Breaking Down a Big Task

**Context:** Someone asks you to estimate something enormous. You can't, and you shouldn't try.

**Good way:**
```
I can't put a number on the whole thing, but I can break it
down: the API is two days, the UI maybe three, the migration
I have no idea until I see the data. Should we start with the
first two and estimate the migration after?
```
✅ Estimate the parts you know, name the part you don't, and propose a sequence.

## Phrases

### Giving a range

```
Somewhere between two and four days.
Two days if it goes well, closer to a week if not.
I'd say three days, give or take.
Roughly a week.
Ballpark: about two sprints.
```

### Signalling confidence

```
I'm fairly confident on this one.
That's a rough guess — don't plan around it yet.
I'd say 80% confident.
This is a real estimate, not a guess.
Take this as a first approximation.
```

### Naming assumptions

```
That assumes the API already supports it.
This is assuming no surprises in the legacy code.
If the schema is what I think it is, then...
The number depends on whether we need a migration.
Best case that's X, worst case it's Y because...
```

### Dependencies and blockers

```
My part is two days, but it's blocked on X.
That's work time, not calendar time.
I can't start until the review lands.
The critical path here is the data team, not me.
It depends on when we get access to staging.
```

### Buying time before committing

```
Can I come back to you with a real number tomorrow?
Let me spend an hour looking at it first.
I'd like to check something before I commit to a date.
I need a spike before I can estimate this.
```

### Pushing back on a deadline

```
I'd push back on that one.
That's the happy path — realistically it's closer to...
I can hit Friday if we drop X. Otherwise it's Tuesday.
That's tight. What can we cut?
```

### Reporting a slip

```
Heads up — I'm going to need two more days.
I'm behind on this one, here's why...
Update on the timeline: ...
I'd rather tell you now than on Friday.
```

---

## Key Rules

1. **Give a range, never a single number**
   - "Three to five days" is honest. "Four days" is a story.
2. **Always attach the assumption**
   - The estimate is only valid if the assumption holds. Say both.
3. **Separate work time from calendar time**
   - "Two days of work, blocked until Thursday" is two different facts.
4. **"I need to look first" is a valid answer**
   - Better than a number you invented in four seconds.
5. **Report slips early, not on the deadline**
   - The delay is forgivable. The surprise isn't.
6. **Push back at the moment, not afterwards**
   - Agreeing to a bad deadline in the meeting and complaining later helps nobody.
7. **Don't apologize repeatedly**
   - "I need two more days, here's why" is enough. Three sorries make it worse.
8. **Estimate the parts, not the whole**
   - When the thing is too big, break it down out loud. That itself is the answer.

---

## What NOT to Do

| ❌ Don't say | ✅ Say instead |
|---|---|
| "Um... three days?" | "Rough guess: three to five. Let me confirm tomorrow." |
| "I don't know." | "I need half a day to look before I can estimate." |
| "Two days." (ignoring the blocker) | "Two days of work, but blocked on the data team." |
| "Yeah, should be fine." (when it isn't) | "I'd push back — realistically it's four to five." |
| "Soon." / "Almost done." | "Wednesday, assuming the review lands today." |
| *Silence until the deadline* | "Heads up, I need two more days. Here's why." |
| "Sorry, sorry, I'm so sorry..." | "I'm behind. Here's the new date." |

---

## The Bigger Picture

An estimate isn't a promise, it's information under uncertainty. My job is to hand over both parts — the number *and* how much to trust it.

The developers I trust most aren't the ones who hit every date. They're the ones whose estimates come with the assumptions attached, so I know what to watch.

That's what I'm trying to sound like in English.
