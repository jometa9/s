date: "20-10-2026"
title: "Studying doesn't work for me — so I'm going to stop"
---

One posts ago I built a study plan. Thirteen lessons, one topic each, made to get my English ready for remote work. Every day I stop my real work, sit down, and study one piece of it.

And every session ends with the same feeling: this doesn't work.

The strange part is that my English *is* getting better. Better words, fewer mistakes, and I've stopped translating in my head before I type. But when I look for where that came from, it wasn't the thirteen lessons. It was the rest of the day — the hours I spend just living in English without calling it "study."

So here's the question I couldn't avoid: if the studying isn't the part that's working, why am I still doing it?

This matters because I could burn a year on the wrong thing. The study sessions are the part that feels like failure — memorizing, repeating, never understanding how the whole thing connects. That feeling is exactly what makes people quit. So I'd be draining myself on the method that doesn't move the needle, and treating the method that does like a side effect.

Then I saw it. I don't need to study English like a university exam. I need to *exist* in English. The growth never came from the lessons — it came from the reps I never scheduled: reading, writing, thinking, working, all of it in English, all day.

So here's what I'm doing:

- No more study sessions. The thirteen-lesson plan is done.
- My whole day runs in English, as far as I can push it. Every action I can move over, I move over.
- I surround myself with it — what I read, what I write, what I listen to.
- When it feels uncomfortable, I stay in it. That discomfort *is* the rep. It's how the action turns automatic.

The bet is simple. For the next month: zero scheduled study sessions, and every day lived in English as much as I can. I'll know it worked when English stops feeling like a mode I switch into — when I stop noticing I'm in it at all.
