With my schedule, taking trades during the New York session is a little complex — sometimes I'm in meetings, sometimes I'm deep in work. ### Market structure

What the market has already done — the first filter.

#### Define it in one sentence

Market structure is the sequence of swing highs and swing lows that shows whether the market is advancing, declining, or consolidating.

It's the first filter because every trade must sit inside a broader narrative. A bullish setup appearing inside a strong bearish structure is lower quality, unless the trade is specifically a reversal model.

#### The four swing points

Every market movement is built from four labels: **HH** (higher high), **HL** (higher low), **LH** (lower high), **LL** (lower low).

- **Uptrend** — a series of higher highs and higher lows. Protects higher lows, expands through prior highs.
- **Downtrend** — a series of lower highs and lower lows. Protects lower highs, expands through prior lows.
- **Ranging** — equal highs and equal lows. Repeated failure to expand cleanly, constant reversion into the middle.

#### BOS vs CHoCH

Two types of break, and you must be able to tell them apart.

**BOS — Break of Structure (continuation).** Price breaks a high, prints a HH, pulls back to a HL, then breaks the high again. Each break says the same thing: the trend continues, buyers are still in power.

**CHoCH — Change of Character (reversal).** Price was in an uptrend, then a low fails to hold and price prints a LL. That's the first sign the other side is coming into power. Once the CHoCH is in, expect price to pull back, form a LH, and sell off into an even lower low.

#### Structure alone is descriptive — displacement gives it intent

Structure tells you what has happened. Displacement tells you what is likely to happen next. One without the other is incomplete information.

Do not judge structure by a single candle break. A clean structural break is far more meaningful when it:

1. occurs with displacement,
2. leaves an inefficiency behind,
3. and takes liquidity that was resting at an obvious swing point.

#### Internal vs external structure

Not all highs and lows carry equal weight.

**External structure** — the swing highs and lows that define the broader range. The largest stop clusters, visible on every timeframe. This is where obvious liquidity accumulates.

**Internal structure** — smaller pivots inside a larger swing. They reveal short-term order flow and are used to time entries *within* an already-established directional move.

A common error is treating every broken candle high or low as a structure shift. Three questions before you believe a break:

1. **Was the level obvious enough to attract liquidity?** Did participants actually have stops resting there?
2. **Did price break it with urgency?** Not a slow drift above or below — a decisive, large-range push.
3. **Did the break create or respect a meaningful imbalance?** A FVG, a S&D origin, a clear inefficiency the market may revisit.

If the answer is no to any of these, the break is probably noise.

#### The three-layer read

| Layer | Job |
|---|---|
| **HTF** — higher timeframe | sets directional bias — the story across recent sessions |
| **ETF** — execution timeframe | reveals where price is interacting with current session levels |
| **TRG** — trigger timeframe | confirms whether the precise entry level is being defended right now |

#### MSS — the full sequence

A market structure shift is the moment one side stops controlling delivery and the opposing side takes initiative. Knowing whether you're trading a **shift** or a **continuation** changes where you look for entries, where the stop goes, and what the target is.

##### Bullish MSS

1. Price sweeps sell-side liquidity below an obvious equal low or swing low.
2. Strong rejection — price closes back above the swept level with force.
3. Displacement breaks an internal lower high, leaving a fair value gap.
4. Price begins to hold higher lows — the shift is confirmed.
5. DR/IDR pullback entries now fit into the bullish continuation that follows.

##### Bearish MSS

1. Price sweeps buy-side liquidity above an obvious equal high or swing high.
2. Strong rejection — price closes back below the swept level with force.
3. Displacement breaks an internal higher low, leaving a fair value gap.
4. Price begins to hold lower highs — the shift is confirmed.
5. DR/IDR pullback entries now fit into the bearish continuation that follows.

**Continuation is different.** The directional narrative is already established. You are not asking who seized control — you are asking where the existing trend will rebalance before expanding again. Most DR/IDR pullback entries belong to this category.

### Premium & discount

The bias behind every trade.

#### Define it in one sentence

Any range has a midpoint. Above that midpoint is premium — expensive. Below it is discount — cheap. Smart money sells premium and buys discount.

Everything else in this guide is built on top of this idea.

#### The 50% equilibrium line

* image here *

#### How to draw it

1. Identify a clear range — swing high to swing low.
2. Mark the midpoint. That's equilibrium (50%).
3. Anything above 50% is premium. Anything below 50% is discount.
4. The range itself can be intraday, daily, or weekly — the same rule applies.

#### Institutions don't chase price

They source liquidity. Large positions can't be filled at one price. Institutions need pools of opposing orders to build size without moving the market against themselves.

**Premium = where buyers cluster.** Retail traders chase strength. They buy breakouts at the top of the range, leaving stops below. Premium becomes a pool of long-side liquidity, perfect for institutions to sell into.

**Discount = where sellers cluster.** The opposite: retail panics on weakness, sells the lows, places stops above. Discount becomes a pool of short-side liquidity — perfect for institutions to buy into.

#### The order flow logic

##### PREMIUM — where institutions distribute

- Retail buys breakouts → creates buy liquidity
- Stops sit below → fuel for the move down
- Institutions sell into this
- Selling at premium = selling expensive
- Result: distribution → expansion lower

##### DISCOUNT — where institutions accumulate

- Retail panics, sells lows → creates sell liquidity
- Stops sit above → fuel for the move up
- Institutions buy into this weakness quietly
- Buying at discount = buying cheap
- Result: accumulation → expansion higher

#### Choosing the right reference range

Premium and discount are relative. The first decision is always: which range am I measuring against?

##### HIGHER TIMEFRAME

Daily / weekly range. Used for directional bias. Where is price within the larger structure? This sets the trade's overall lean — long bias if daily is in discount, short bias if daily is in premium.

##### INTERMEDIATE

4H / 1H range. Used for setup confirmation.

##### LOWER TIMEFRAME

Session / DR range. Used for execution. The London or NY session range gives you the precise level to act on. This is where DR/IDR operates — and why it works.

#### The fib levels that actually matter

Equilibrium (50%) is the line. But the levels you execute from sit deeper than that.

Plot the fib tool:

- **For longs (discount):** swing low → swing high.
- **For shorts (premium):** swing high → swing low.

The levels of interest are **0.705, 0.79 and 0.886**. Price at exactly 50% is neither expensive nor cheap — there is no edge in executing there. That is why a textbook zone sitting at equilibrium so often fails while the deeper one behind it holds: the 50% zone gets used as liquidity to reach the 0.705/0.79 zone.

#### The most common location error

Most losing trades are **directionally correct but locationally wrong.**

The trader reads bullish order flow correctly, then buys in premium — directly beneath resting buy-side liquidity. That liquidity gets swept first, the long gets stopped out, and the real bullish move begins without them.

Same in reverse: correctly bearish, but shorting in discount, directly above resting sell-side liquidity.

Direction is only half the trade. Location is the other half.

#### Premium/discount does not guarantee a reversal

It is a filter, not a signal. It tells you whether price is attractively located — nothing more.

Location only increases quality when it is combined with structure and time. A bearish signal in premium, during an active killzone, after liquidity has been swept, is worth far more than simply noticing that price is in the upper half of a range.

#### The module in 5 lines

- Every range has a 50% midpoint — that's equilibrium.
- Above 50% is premium. Below 50% is discount.
- Fib levels: 0, 0.5, 0.705, 0.79, 1.
- Always read premium/discount on the right timeframe — HTF for bias, LTF for execution.
- This is the bias filter that gates every other module in this guide.

### Supply & demand

Where moves are born and where they die.

#### Define it in one sentence

A supply or demand zone is the price area where institutions left unfilled orders. When price returns, those orders fire — and the move continues.

#### The anatomy of a zone

Every valid zone has the same anatomy.

Price approaches a level, pauses inside a base, then leaves with strong displacement.

The base is where the order block sits. Institutions can't fill their full size at one price — so they accumulate or distribute inside that consolidation. The strong departure proves the orders were real.

If there's no base, or the move out is weak, there's no zone.

#### What's actually happening at a zone

Institutions can't fill at one price. A position size that moves the market needs multiple price points to fill.

Some orders go unfilled. Even with a clean base, not every order gets matched before price departs. Those leftover orders sit at the zone, waiting for price to return so they can be executed.

Return to the zone triggers them. When price revisits the base, the unfilled orders start firing. That re-introduces real institutional flow into the market, which is why the move continues from these levels.

#### The three words behind every zone

Before the patterns, the vocabulary:

- **Rally** — buyers exceed sellers.
- **Base** — buyers and sellers are equal to each other.
- **Drop** — sellers exceed buyers.

Every zone is just those three words in a sequence.

#### The Four Building Blocks

##### RBR — Rally → Base → Rally

**Type:** Continuation Demand

Price rallies, pauses in a base, then rallies again. The base represents unfilled buy orders. Look for long opportunities when price returns to the zone.

##### DBD — Drop → Base → Drop

**Type:** Continuation Supply

Price drops, pauses in a base, then drops again. The base represents unfilled sell orders. Look for short opportunities when price returns to the zone.

##### DBR — Drop → Base → Rally

**Type:** Reversal Demand

Price drops, pauses in a base, then reverses upward. The base marks a shift in sentiment and acts as an order block. Consider it a strong long zone.

##### RBD — Rally → Base → Drop

**Type:** Reversal Supply

Price rallies, pauses in a base, then reverses downward. The base marks a shift in sentiment and acts as an order block. Consider it a strong short zone.

#### How to Draw the Zone Correctly

Rules:

1. Find the Base
Identify the **1–5 candles** where price consolidates before making a strong move away from the area.

2. Box the Base Candles Only
Draw the zone using **only the base candles**:
- Wick to wick of the base candles, **or**
- From the previous candle before the engulfing move (depending on your trading rules).

3. Confirm Strong Departure
The price should leave the zone with a **large-bodied displacement candle**, showing strong buying or selling pressure.

#### The five marks of a high-quality zone

Zones are not magical rectangles. Their value comes from the reaction that followed them. A zone that caused a weak bounce is worth far less than one that launched displacement and broke structure.

The best zones share five characteristics:

1. **HTF aligned** — the zone sits in the direction of the higher timeframe narrative.
2. **Liquidity event** — the zone followed or created a sweep of a meaningful pool.
3. **Displacement** — the departure was strong and urgent, not a gradual drift.
4. **FVG left** — the departure left a fair value gap or visible imbalance behind.
5. **Sensible location** — demand in discount for buys, supply in premium for sells.

Five out of five is the benchmark. Anything under three is not a setup, it's a rectangle.

#### Don't draw it too wide — and don't over-refine it either

Two opposite mistakes, same result.

**Too broad** — the wider the zone, the less precise the execution and the worse the risk/reward.

**Too refined** — if you refine so tightly that normal market noise stops you out before the idea is actually invalidated, your precision has become fragility.

The practice: start from the source of displacement, then refine only when the lower timeframe supports the refinement.

One useful refinement is the **indecision candle** — a candle with a small body and mostly wicks, where the body closes near the middle. Both sides had equal power there. Drawing the zone off that candle instead of the whole base gives a tighter, more meaningful area.

#### Filtering with Premium / Discount

1 — Locate It
Determine whether the zone is in a **Higher Time Frame (HTF) Premium** or **HTF Discount** area. Map it to your HTF range before considering a trade.

2 — Find the Zone
Identify a clean Supply & Demand pattern:
- RBR (Rally → Base → Rally)
- DBD (Drop → Base → Drop)
- DBR (Drop → Base → Rally)
- RBD (Rally → Base → Drop)

The pattern should have a well-defined base and a strong departure.

3 — Trade Only with Bias
Only trade zones that align with your market bias:
- **Premium + Bearish bias →** Look for Supply zones (shorts).
- **Discount + Bullish bias →** Look for Demand zones (longs).

#### Module Summary

1. A Supply or Demand zone is an area where institutions left unfilled orders during a base.
2. The four core patterns are:
   - **RBR** and **DBD** → Continuation patterns.
   - **DBR** and **RBD** → Reversal (Order Block) patterns.
3. Draw the zone using only the base candles (the order block).
4. Filter every zone using the Premium/Discount framework before trading.
5. Focus only on fresh zones with strong departures that are located in the correct Premium or Discount area.

### Imbalance — FVG & displacement

Where price moved too fast, and where it comes back.

#### Define it in one sentence

A fair value gap is a three-candle imbalance where the market repriced so quickly that the first and third candles do not fully overlap.

Same idea, three names: **fair value gap (FVG)**, **imbalance**, **market inefficiency**. Traders use different words for the same thing.

#### Efficient vs inefficient price action

- **Efficient** — the wicks of candle 1 and candle 3 meet. Price gave both buyers and sellers a fair shot at accessing liquidity.
- **Inefficient** — the wicks do not meet. There's a gap. One side overwhelmed the other and price never traded fairly through that area.

Inefficiency means the move was caused by large orders pumped in at that point. If price impulsed up and left imbalance behind, a lot of buyers came into power at once — and vice versa.

Because that area was never traded fairly, the market often returns to rebalance part of it before continuing. That return is your opportunity to enter.

But: **not every imbalance gets filled.** Do not treat an unfilled gap as a promise.

#### Not every gap is worth trading

The best fair value gaps are:

- born from displacement,
- aligned with higher timeframe direction,
- formed **after** a liquidity event,
- situated in sensible price territory (discount for longs, premium for shorts).

A weak FVG inside random consolidation is low quality. A FVG that forms immediately after sweeping session liquidity and breaking structure is meaningful.

#### The FVG is an execution mechanism, not a signal

This is the distinction that matters most.

The FVG tells you **where** to enter. It does not tell you **why** to enter.

Structure, liquidity and displacement establish the *why* first. The FVG then provides the precise entry location inside an already-established directional narrative. If the intention is clear, the gap gives you precision. If the intention is unclear, the gap becomes a trap.

#### Displacement — the proof of intent

Displacement is the strong, forceful move away from a level or through a structure point, usually leaving an imbalance behind.

The significance is **not candle size. It's urgency.** Urgency means one side was willing to pay away from fair value to get positioned or exit aggressively.

A large candle that grinds slowly through a level over many minutes is not the same as a candle that cuts through the same level in two minutes. The second has urgency. The first may be noise.

##### What true displacement looks like

- Large-range candles closing near their extremes.
- A decisive break through an obvious level in 1–2 candles, not a grind.
- A visible fair value gap left in its wake.
- The move is unmistakable.

> If you have to convince yourself it counts — it probably doesn't.

This is why the DR/IDR model doesn't buy every touch of a range low or sell every touch of a range high. You want evidence the market has already chosen a direction. Displacement is that evidence: it says the market is not merely rotating inside the range, it's trying to leave it.

### Power of three

#### Define it in one sentence

Every move price makes follows the same three-step story: it accumulates, it manipulates, then it distributes.

The same shape repeats — on the daily candle, on a 1H candle, even on a 5-minute candle.

#### The three phases

##### A — ACCUMULATION

Build

Price coils into a tight range. Institutions quietly load up positions. The market looks indecisive — but it's already setting the trap.

##### M — MANIPULATION

Trap

Price expands violently into one side of the range — sweeping liquidity. Retail enters thinking it's the trend. It isn't. It's the fake-out.

##### D — DISTRIBUTION

Deliver

Price reverses and runs the opposite direction — the true move of the cycle. The manipulation provided the fuel; this is the burn.

#### WHY IT WORKS

##### Institutions need liquidity to fill

Big positions can't be filled at one price. The accumulation phase quietly builds the position. The manipulation phase generates the counter-orders needed to complete it.

##### Retail provides the fuel

When price expands aggressively, retail traders chase. Their entries (and stops) become the liquidity pool that institutions use to fill the rest of their position — at a better price.

##### Distribution is the actual move

Once the position is filled, price runs in the opposite direction. The manipulation was setup. Distribution is the trade. Most retail is on the wrong side by the time it starts.

#### HOW TO IDENTIFY IT

Spotting each phase live

##### A — ACCUMULATION

WHAT YOU SEE

Tight range. Small candles. Volume contracting. Price respecting both sides of a narrow band.

WHAT IT MEANS

Position-building in progress. The algorithm is loading. No edge in trading inside this range.

##### M — MANIPULATION

WHAT YOU SEE

Aggressive expansion through one side of the accumulation range. Often a sweep of an obvious high or low.

WHAT IT MEANS

Liquidity raid. Retail just got trapped on the wrong side. Bias should now flip — opposite of where the sweep went.

##### D — DISTRIBUTION

WHAT YOU SEE

Sustained displacement back through the accumulation range, toward the opposite side. Strong, one-directional flow.

WHAT IT MEANS

The actual move. This is where the trade lives — entries are taken at the manipulation extreme, targeting opposite liquidity.

#### The daily profiles — what they are

The four profiles below are **narrative models, not signals.** Not strategies, not entries. They are frameworks for reading the algorithm's intent across the day — they are recognitions, not predictions.

Every profile answers the same three questions:

1. **Where was liquidity accumulated?** — typically Asia, the accumulation zone.
2. **Where was liquidity manipulated?** — London or NY, depending on the profile.
3. **Which session performs true distribution?** — the opposite of the manipulation session.

This is the real utility: the profiles show Power of Three applied not to a single candle, but to the full trading day.

#### PROFILE 01

London Manip.

NY Distribution.

The foundational daily profile. Most common in algorithmic delivery.

##### STRUCTURE

- Asia forms a stable, narrow range.
- London violates one side of that range — high or low.
- New York completes delivery by attacking the opposite side.

**ASIA → LONDON SWEEP → NY DELIVERY**

#### PROFILE 01 OF 04 — LOGIC

Why this profile is the foundation

London represents the manipulation phase — a deliberate expansion beyond Asia's liquidity to create an artificial directional bias. Retail enters the London move thinking it's the trend. It isn't. It's the trap.

New York then represents the distribution phase — the true delivery of the daily candle, using the liquidity that London engineered. The algorithm inverts on the opposite side and walks price toward the real target.

##### THE MECHANISM

London provides the wick of the daily candle. NY provides the body. Asia is the engineered liquidity pool that fuels both.

##### THE TAKEAWAY

When Asia consolidates tightly and London opens with aggressive expansion through one side, your bias should immediately tilt toward this profile. The opposite side of Asia's range becomes the true target — not the side London violated. Recognise the trap and you recognise the day. 

#### PROFILE 02

NY Manipulation + Continuation.

Single-session PO3, localized inside New York.

##### STRUCTURE

- Asia and London form a shared consolidation — coiled liquidity.
- New York sweeps one side of this combined range.
- Instead of reversing, price continues in the same direction after the sweep.

**ASIA + LONDON → NY SWEEP → NY EXPANSION**

##### THE LOGIC

Here manipulation and distribution both happen in New York, as two separate legs: **NY sweep = manipulation, NY expansion = distribution.**

Asia and London quietly accumulate in a tight, coiled range — the first two sessions store energy. NY becomes the central point of intent and executes both halves of the day's narrative in one window.

##### THE CRITICAL INSIGHT

The sweep does **not** signal a reversal. It is fuel for continuation. The algorithm has chosen a trend day, not a reversal day.

##### HOW TO READ IT

The tell is the consolidation. When Asia and London both refuse to expand — when the whole pre-NY session looks like it's drawing breath — the algorithm is preparing a single dominant directional move.

Treat the sweep as the start of the move, not the end of it.

#### PROFILE 03

The Death Profile.

The clearest expression of unidirectional bearish control.

##### STRUCTURE

- Each new session fails to exceed the previous session's high.
- Each new session does break the previous session's low.
- The day forms lower highs and lower lows, session by session.

**ASIA → LONDON LL → NY LL**

##### THE LOGIC

There is no rotation and no manipulation phase to flip the day. Every session does the same thing: refuses to take buy-side liquidity and continuously targets sell-side liquidity. The algorithm is compressing premium → discount across every session.

The daily candle is almost always an **OHLC structure** — open near the high, close near the low. Pure bearish delivery.

##### HOW TO READ IT

This profile is unforgiving for counter-trend reads. Every push back into premium looks like the start of a reversal — none of them are. Each push up is engineered fuel for the next leg down.

Recognising the profile early is what protects you from fading it.

#### PROFILE 04

The Trend Profile.

The purest form of bullish algorithmic delivery. The positive version of the Death Profile.

##### STRUCTURE

- Each new session fails to take the previous session's low.
- Each new session does take the previous session's high.
- The day forms higher highs and higher lows all day.

**ASIA → LONDON HH → NY HH**

##### THE LOGIC

Instead of manipulation → reversal, the day is minor consolidations → expansions → consolidations → expansions. Each pause is a re-accumulation, each expansion a continuation. Sell-side liquidity is respected at every turn — it's the foundation the algorithm builds the trend on.

The daily candle is almost always an **OLHC structure** — open near the low, close near the high.

##### HOW TO READ IT

Every session pullback holds. The market is not asking permission, it's executing. Counter-trend reads don't survive long.

#### Cross-profile logic

All four profiles are fractal variations of the same accumulation → manipulation → distribution. The unit changes — sometimes the full day, sometimes a single session — but the algorithm is the same.

| Profile | Daily candle form | Algorithmic intent |
|---|---|---|
| London Manip → NY Distribution | Hybrid (wick + body across sessions) | Wants a reversal day |
| NY Manip + Continuation | Hybrid (PO3 localized in NY) | Wants a trend day from NY |
| Death Profile | OHLC (pure bearish) | Wants aggressive sell-side efficiency |
| Trend Profile | OLHC (pure bullish) | Wants aggressive buy-side efficiency |

Every profile expresses the same theme:

**ENGINEERED LIQUIDITY → LIQUIDITY RAID → TARGET LIQUIDITY**

What changes is which session does what. Identify the role each session plays and you've identified the profile. Identify the profile and you've identified the day.

### Killzones

The hours of the day that actually matter.

#### Define it in one sentence

A killzone is a specific window of the trading day during which institutional volume is highest — and when manipulation, displacement, and reversals are most likely to occur.

Outside of killzones, the market drifts. Inside them, the algorithm executes.

#### The trading day

Each killzone has a different role. Treat them like four different markets in one day. These are the windows when institutional volume peaks.

| Killzone | NY time | ARG — NY winter<br>(Nov → Mar) | ARG — NY summer<br>(Mar → Nov) | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ASIA** | 20:00 — 00:00 | 22:00 — 02:00 | 21:00 — 01:00 | **Accumulation.** Slow, range-bound, narrow. The day's range is being set. Don't trade — observe |
| **LONDON** | 02:00 — 05:00 | 04:00 — 07:00 | 03:00 — 06:00 | **Manipulation** (typical). Often the day's first sweep. The Asia range gets violated to engineer liquidity. The trap is being set |
| **NY AM** | 07:00 — 10:00 | 09:00 — 12:00 | 08:00 — 11:00 | **Distribution** (typical). Highest volume of the day. The true move usually delivers here |
| **LONDON CLOSE** | 10:00 — 12:00 | 12:00 — 14:00 | 11:00 — 13:00 | **Re-delivery.** London desks closing into NY. Continuation, profit-taking, or a counter-move into the daily mean |

Three of the four are clustered around the London and New York sessions. Asia is the accumulation zone — quieter, slower, the warm-up. The action starts when London opens.

##### Why there are two columns — the one-hour seasonal shift

This is the detail that quietly breaks people's charts twice a year, so it's worth being explicit about.

**Argentina does not observe daylight saving time. New York does.** The market's schedule is anchored to New York, not to me — so the killzones don't move in NY time, they move in *my* time. Twice a year the whole map slides by one hour while I'm sitting in the same chair.

| Period | Months | NY offset | My conversion | London ODR in my time |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **NY winter** | early **November → early March**<br>(Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb + first days of Mar) | EST, UTC-5 | ARG = NY **+2** | **05:00 — 06:00** |
| **NY summer** | early **March → early November**<br>(Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct) | EDT, UTC-4 | ARG = NY **+1** | **04:00 — 05:00** |

Roughly: **eight months of the year I wake at 04:00, four months I wake at 05:00.** The longer stretch is the early one — March through October is the 04:00 period, November through February the 05:00 one.

The exact switch dates move every year because they're defined by weekday, not date:

| Change | When | Effect on me |
|---|---|---|
| **Spring forward** | second Sunday of **March** | everything moves **one hour earlier** — ODR now 04:00 |
| **Fall back** | first Sunday of **November** | everything moves **one hour later** — ODR back to 05:00 |

So the two Mondays to have in the calendar are the one right after the second Sunday of March, and the one right after the first Sunday of November. Those are the mornings people show up at the wrong hour and draw the wrong range.

One extra wrinkle worth knowing: these dates do **not** line up with the European changeover (the UK switches on the last Sunday of March and the last Sunday of October). For a few weeks each year — roughly the second half of March, and the last week of October — London and New York are on a different offset than usual, so the session overlaps sit differently. It doesn't change the model, but it's the explanation when the chart feels an hour off during those specific weeks.

**The practical rule:** from here on, every market window in this post is given in **New York time**, because that's what it's actually anchored to — the ODR is the 03:00 NY candle in January and in July. Only my alarm clock moves. When I mention my own hours (waking up, being at the charts), those are ARG, and the tables above convert between the two.

This is also the honest answer to "why 4 or 5 ARG depending on the season" from the beginning of the post. It's not vagueness. It's the same 03:00 NY candle, reached from a country that doesn't move its clocks.

> If you trade from a different timezone: don't memorise my numbers. Take the **NY column**, apply your own offset, and check whether your country observes DST. The killzone is defined by New York's clock — everything else is arithmetic.

#### Why time, not just price, matters

##### Liquidity is concentrated

Institutional desks are active during specific session overlaps. Outside those hours, volume thins and price drifts. The same setup that fires during NY AM might do nothing at 01:00 ARG.

##### Killzones are when traps spring

PO3 manipulation phases need liquidity to fire. The biggest pools form when retail is active — which is during major sessions. That's why killzones are when the trap is set.

##### Setups outside killzones underperform

A perfect S&D zone tapped during off-hours often does nothing.

#### Time creates permission

A valid-looking pattern outside a killzone may still work occasionally, but the same pattern inside the proper session has better logic behind it — because the market actually has a reason to move then.

You are not only looking for the right pattern. You are looking for **the right pattern at the right time.**

Certain windows are more likely to form ranges. Others are more likely to raid those ranges. Others are more likely to deliver continuation after a retracement. That's why the session map comes before the trade idea, not after it.

#### The Asian range

The overnight range that forms before London becomes active. In practice it functions as the accumulation zone — where liquidity builds quietly before the aggressive participants arrive. The Asian high and Asian low are the key reference points, because London usually interacts with them early.

The exact time definition varies between traders and templates. Because of that, **the only acceptable approach is consistency**: once you choose a window and normalise it to UTC-3, apply it the same way every single day. The point is not to win an argument about the correct window — it's to identify the overnight range whose extremes hold resting liquidity.

##### How to use it — four steps

1. **Mark the high and the low.** These are your overnight reference levels. Asian high = potential BSL. Asian low = potential SSL.
2. **Observe whether London sweeps a side.** A sweep with a strong wick followed by reversal is the first evidence of directional intent.
3. **Assess alignment with HTF bias.** A sweep in the direction that removes obstacles for the HTF bias is higher quality than one that fights it.
4. **Look for displacement and structure reclaim.** Sweep + rejection + displacement + reclaim = the Asian range is now a high-conviction intraday reference. That is the ideal sequence.

### DR/IDR

Where bias, levels, narrative and timing become a single trade.

#### Define it in one sentence

DR/IDR is the framework for trading the breakout-and-retrace inside a session: the DR is the balance, the breakout reveals intent, and the DR levels are where you enter on the pullback.

Don't trade the breakout. Trade the retracement after it.

#### DR and IDR

##### DR — DEFINED RANGE

The balance.

The high and low formed during a specific 60-minute window. London ODR (03:00—04:00 NY) and NY RDR (09:30—10:30 NY) are the two DRs you'll work with. See [the season table below](#the-dr-windows-across-the-season) for what those are in my clock.

The DR usually captures the session's accumulation.

##### IDR — IMPLIED DEFINED RANGE

The internal pricing logic of the DR — the boundary, the FVG and the midpoint used to frame pullback entries after the breakout.

DR gives you the balance phase. The breakout gives you the imbalance phase. IDR frames the retracement. **That sequence — balance, imbalance, retracement — is the backbone of the whole strategy.**

#### Why London uses the first hour but NY uses 09:30–10:30

Not an arbitrary choice. A model choice. Both windows are defined in **New York time**, because that's what they're anchored to — expressing them in my clock hides the reason they exist.

**London ODR — Opening Defined Range (03:00–04:00 NY).** London's opening behaviour is immediately important for price delivery. Its first hour can directly attack overnight liquidity and set the session tone.

**NY RDR — Redefining Range (09:30–10:30 NY).** New York is *not* framed from the first forex-session print. It is anchored to the **cash equity open** — 09:30 is the opening bell — because that is where participation and volatility become meaningful enough to build a range worth trading.

Consequence: the NY DR can never be traded in a vacuum. Before its breakout, you should already know what London accomplished. Did London already sweep the daily draw? Did it leave unfilled inefficiencies? Did it overextend into external liquidity? If NY forms a range after London delivered a one-sided move, the breakout quality has to be judged in light of that.

> **Critical principle:** a Defined Range should capture *the range that matters*, not merely the first possible range on the chart. A useless first hour creates a useless range. Always ask: did this range form in a context where a subsequent breakout makes structural sense?

##### The DR windows across the season

The DR is the one place where getting the hour wrong doesn't just cost you a trade — it draws you a completely different range, and every level derived from it is then wrong for the rest of the day. Both windows are anchored to New York, so in my clock they shift by one hour twice a year:

| Defined Range | NY time | ARG — NY winter<br>**Nov → Mar** | ARG — NY summer<br>**Mar → Nov** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **London ODR** | 03:00 — 04:00 | 05:00 — 06:00 | 04:00 — 05:00 |
| **NY RDR** | 09:30 — 10:30 | 11:30 — 12:30 | 10:30 — 11:30 |

The London ODR is the one I actually trade, so in practice:

- **March to October** (the long stretch, 8 months) — charts open **04:00 ARG**, range closes **05:00 ARG**
- **November to February** (4 months) — charts open **05:00 ARG**, range closes **06:00 ARG**

The safest habit is to set your charting platform to display New York time — then the ODR is always the 03:00 candle regardless of the season, and the only thing that changes is what time your alarm goes off.

#### Anatomy of a DR

##### 01 — DR High & Low (Wick to Wick)

The highest wick and lowest wick during the 60-minute window.

##### 02 — IDR High & Low (Body to Body)

The highest body & lowest body during the 60-minute window.

##### 03 — Midpoint

The 50% level — the equilibrium of the range.

#### The Three Key Entries

Once price breaks out of the DR, three pullback levels become the candidates for entry.

##### TIER 1 — PRIMARY

Supply/Demand & FVG

Zones within the 60-minute window are extremely probable, especially when aligned with the range's key levels.

##### TIER 2 — SECONDARY

DR Structure

The DR high & low become critical areas for entry, and can be used as support & resistance.

##### TIER 3 — DEEP RETRACE

DR Midpoint (50%)

The midpoint is also an area to monitor — watch how price reacts from it.

#### The breakout sequence

##### O1 — DR forms

Price ranges inside the 60-minute window. The high and low become the DR Levels.

##### O2 — Breakout

Price violates one side of the DR with displacement. This reveals intent — the algorithm has chosen direction.

##### O3 — Retracement

Price pulls back into the DR range — to your zone, FVG, DR Levels, or midpoint.

##### O4 — Entry & continuation

Entry triggers at one of those levels. Price then continues in the direction of the breakout.

#### The DR live — THE RULES

##### Wait for the window to close

Wait for **04:00 NY** (London ODR) or **10:30 NY** (NY RDR). The range is not the range until the window has closed.

##### High to low — wick to wick

The wick high and wick low of the 60 minutes are the DR. The body high and body low are the IDR.

##### Extend the levels right

Extend the DR high, low, and midpoint forward across the rest of the session.

#### Trading the retracement

##### O1 — Wait for the pullback

After the breakout, price needs to retrace into the DR. Don't chase the breakout candle. Sit and wait.

##### O2 — Identify the key level

Where is price pulling back to?

##### O3 — Enter with confluence

Candle rejections, etc.

#### Breakout quality

The best breakout is not simply a candle that closes outside the range. It has qualities:

| Mark | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Decisive** | 1–2 candle break with urgency — not a slow grind above/below the boundary |
| **Liquidity** | Takes or responds to a nearby BSL/SSL pool before or during the break |
| **FVG left** | Leaves a visible fair value gap or imbalance behind |
| **HTF aligned** | Consistent with the higher timeframe narrative — not breaking into major opposing context |

##### Weak breakout warning signs

- The candle closes just outside the range and immediately wicks back in.
- Multiple small candles grind above/below the boundary instead of one decisive break.
- The breakout occurs directly into a prior HTF supply or demand zone.
- No fair value gap is created.

The point of the model is **not** to trade every breakout. It's to trade the breakout that reveals true intent, then wait for the refined pullback.

#### The two-step entry logic

**Step 1:** the DR defines the *area* to watch for a pullback — range boundary, FVG, or midpoint.
**Step 2:** the lower timeframe tells you *when* that area is being actively defended.

The entry is only taken when both conditions are met. Never enter blind into a zone without LTF confirmation.

A shallow retracement into the range boundary is often a sign of strength — the market barely gave the level back before continuing. A deeper retracement into the midpoint can still be valid if the narrative is intact, but needs extra confirmation. A retracement deep into the full opposite side of the range weakens the continuation case significantly.

#### Targets

Targets come from the chart, not from emotion. The advantage of targeting liquidity rather than arbitrary pip counts is that it aligns your exits with market purpose.

- **T1 — the nearest credible draw.** The closest visible BSL (longs) or SSL (shorts) that price has a logical reason to reach. This is the primary exit.
- **T2 — the next major external draw.** Only pursued when structure clearly supports continuation and T1 has been taken or is being approached.
- **Rule-based exits.** If the model says the draw is at a specific level, exit at that level. Do not adjust based on hope or greed.

If the market is moving to take a known pool, exiting before it without reason reduces expectancy. Holding beyond the obvious objective without evidence gives back a well-structured trade.

#### Invalidations

A strategy becomes professional when it knows not just what confirms a trade, but what cancels it. Every trade needs a defined invalidation **before** entry — not after things start going wrong.

- **Breakout invalidation** — the breakout lacked displacement; the candle was weak, small, or immediately reversed back inside the range.
- **Pullback invalidation** — price cannot hold the pullback zone; it pushes through the FVG, boundary or midpoint with no lower timeframe defense.
- **Structure invalidation** — the LTF fails to produce the expected structure shift or continuation signal after price reaches the zone.
- **Re-acceptance invalidation** — the market re-enters the DR and accepts price on the opposite side, ranging and distributing back through the range against the breakout direction.

> If the market has re-entered the range, accepted the opposite side, and no longer shows urgency in your favour — the trade is no longer the trade you planned. Holding it hoping for recovery is not trading the model. It's trading hope.

### Liquidity

#### Define it in one sentence

Liquidity is just an area where stop losses and pending orders sit. Smart money hunts it because it's the only fuel large enough to fill institutional positions.

Once you see liquidity, you stop seeing patterns. You start seeing intent.

#### We are all liquidity

Every stop loss you place is somebody else's entry. Every pending order is somebody else's target. We are all liquidity in the market.

##### BUT NOT EVERY POOL GETS GRABBED

Some pools simply aren't liquidated enough for it to be worth the grab.

Smart money doesn't sweep everything. It targets pools large enough to fill real institutional size — that's why obvious, well-watched levels matter most. The bigger and more obvious the pool, the more likely it gets taken. Tiny, isolated stops? Often left alone.

#### Buy-side vs sell-side liquidity

Every pool of liquidity sits on one of two sides. That's the whole binary.

##### BSL — BUY-SIDE LIQUIDITY

Above the highs.

Stops from short positions sit above swing highs. Buy-stop orders from breakout traders sit there too.

When price runs above a high, both groups get triggered — generating buy orders that smart money sells into. The liquidity is on the buy side.

##### SSL — SELL-SIDE LIQUIDITY

Below the lows.

Stops from long positions sit below swing lows. Sell-stop orders from breakdown traders sit there too.

When price drops below a low, both groups get triggered — generating sell orders that smart money buys into. The liquidity is on the sell side.

##### IRL — INTRARANGE LIQUIDITY

The third category, and the one most people forget.

Session highs and lows, prior DR levels, and swing points sitting *inside* the current dealing range. Not the extremes — the furniture in between. These are what price uses as stepping stones on the way to the external pools.

#### The question is not "where is liquidity"

Liquidity should never be read in isolation. The useful question is not *"where is liquidity?"* — that's just marking lines.

The useful question is: **"which liquidity is likely to be taken first, and what is the market likely to do after taking it?"**

That second question is where structure, premium/discount and time come together. Always know which side is nearest, which side has already been taken, and which side is likely to become the next draw on price.

#### The sweep is the signal — not the touch

Price approaching a liquidity level isn't a signal. Price taking the level — and reversing — is.

##### THE TOUCH (NOT A SIGNAL)

Price tags the level and stalls.

No stops triggered. No orders filled. Just price brushing the area. The pool is still sitting there. Don't trade it — wait.

##### THE SWEEP (THE SIGNAL)

Price takes the level — then reverses.

Stops trigger. Orders fill. Smart money has its fuel. The reversal is the proof — that's your entry trigger, not the touch itself.

#### The three retail liquidity pools

Retail traders cluster their orders in three predictable places. Smart money knows this — and feeds on it: **equal highs / lows**, **trendlines**, and **S&D zones at equilibrium**.

##### Equal highs / lows — and their cousins

The same mechanism shows up under several retail names. All of them are the same trap:

- **Double tops** — retail identifies a high, sells the retest, stops a few pips above. The stops are the pool.
- **Double bottoms** — retail identifies a low, buys the retest, stops a few pips below. Same thing, other side.
- **Support / resistance levels** — a level price has tested multiple times. Every touch adds more stops just beyond it.

The pattern is always identical: price moves in the retail trader's direction just far enough to convince them the strategy works, and then takes them out.

##### Trendlines — diagonal liquidity

A trendline is just a sloped line connecting swing points. Retail loves them because they look clean — and that's exactly why they're a liquidity trap.

Traders who buy a rising trendline place their stops just below it. Traders who short a falling trendline place their stops just above. When price violates the line, those stops cascade in a single move. The break of the trendline is a liquidity raid.

The more touches a trendline has, the more obvious it becomes, and the more liquidity stacks behind it. Clean three-touch trendlines almost always get swept before the real move.

##### S&D zones at equilibrium

Not every supply or demand zone is liquidity. The ones that matter most are stacked at the equilibrium of a larger range — the 50% level of the structure.

When retail spots a fresh demand zone in the middle of a range, they buy with stops just below. When they spot a supply zone at equilibrium, they short with stops just above. Either way, equilibrium-level S&D zones become liquidity pools dressed up as setups.

Smart money runs price through these zones to harvest the stops, then reverses from the deeper, more meaningful S&D zone behind them. That's why a textbook zone in the middle of the range often fails — and the deeper one further from equilibrium holds.

#### What a real sweep looks like

##### O1 — Approach

Price moves toward the obvious level. Tension builds. Retail leans in — taking positions, placing stops just beyond.

##### O2 — The take

Price spikes through the level — often as a single fast candle or a sharp wick. Stops fire. Pending orders trigger. Liquidity is harvested.

##### O3 — The reversal

Price closes back inside the prior range, often within minutes. The wick is left behind as evidence. The sweep is now complete — and the real move starts.

If the take happens but price doesn't reverse — that wasn't a sweep. That was real direction. Different play.

#### The module in 5 lines

1. Liquidity = stop losses + pending orders. We are all liquidity in the market.
2. Smart money only targets pools large enough to fill institutional size — the obvious ones win.
3. BSL sits above the highs. SSL sits below the lows. That's the binary.
4. Three retail pools: equal highs/lows, trendlines, S&D zones at equilibrium.
5. The sweep is the signal — not the touch. Wait for the take and the reversal.

### Confluence hierarchy

When several signals appear at once, they are not worth the same.

Not everything that lines up deserves equal weight. This ranking prevents the most common error in the whole model: overvaluing a pretty pattern that formed in poor context.

| # | Signal | Why it ranks here |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **HTF structure** | The foundation. Wrong here and every other signal is built on sand. Always assess it first |
| 2 | **Premium / discount location** | Buying discount in bullish context, selling premium in bearish. Filters bias into price territory |
| 3 | **Liquidity event** | Was a meaningful BSL/SSL pool swept before this setup? The sweep is often the catalyst |
| 4 | **Displacement** | Did price show urgency and intent? No displacement = no evidence of a chosen direction |
| 5 | **Fair value gap** | Is there a clean imbalance to enter into? Formed during displacement, not previously filled |
| 6 | **Session timing** | Is this inside an active killzone? Outside = lower institutional participation |
| 7 | **LTF trigger** | Is the level being defended right now? The final confirmation — not the reason to be in the trade |

A FVG inside premium, during London, after buy-side liquidity was just swept and bearish displacement broke structure, is meaningful. The same FVG in the middle of nowhere, at a random hour, with no prior liquidity event, is just a gap.

#### The 7/7 benchmark

> HTF is bullish. Price in discount. London swept the Asia low (SSL). Displacement broke the ODR high leaving a FVG. The FVG sits at the ODR high, in discount. It's 09:45 NY — inside the NY AM killzone. The 1m shows a bullish MSS off the FVG.

That's 7/7. It's the benchmark every trade should be measured against — not the minimum to take a trade, but the thing you compare against to know how much you're compromising.

## The strategy setup

Everything above is the theory. This is the checklist I actually run, in order, every morning.

The rule that holds it all together: **structure first, location second, liquidity third, imbalance fourth, time fifth, execution last.** If a step fails, there is no trade that day. No forcing.

---

### Before the session — at the charts before the ODR closes (04:00 NY)

**Step 1 — HTF bias.**
Open the daily. Is price in premium or discount of the daily/weekly range? Mark the 50%.
- Daily in discount → I only look for longs
- Daily in premium → I only look for shorts
- Price sitting at equilibrium → no bias, no trade

**Step 2 — map the liquidity.**
Mark on the chart:
- BSL: equal highs, prior session high, obvious swing highs
- SSL: equal lows, prior session low, obvious swing lows
- The Asia range high and low (this is the pool London will attack)

Then ask one question: **which side is untouched, and which one is likely to go first?**

**Step 3 — mark the zones.**
Find the S&D zones (RBR / DBD / DBR / RBD) that sit in the correct half of the range. Demand in discount for longs, supply in premium for shorts. Ignore zones sitting at equilibrium — those are liquidity, not setups.

Also mark unfilled FVGs from the previous session; they act as draws.

**Step 4 — write the narrative in one sentence.**
Before anything moves. For example:

> "Asia is tight, London should sweep the Asia low into discount, then displace up toward the previous day high."

If I can't write the sentence, I don't understand the day. No trade.

---

### During the session

**Step 5 — let the DR form and close.**
London ODR = 03:00 to 04:00 NY (05:00–06:00 my time in NY winter, 04:00–05:00 in NY summer). **Wait for the candle to close.** Do not draw it early.

Then mark:
- DR high / DR low → wick to wick
- IDR high / IDR low → body to body
- Midpoint → 50%

Extend all of them to the right, across the rest of the session.

**Step 6 — wait for the sweep.**
Does price take the Asia high or the Asia low? I want the **take + the reversal**, not the touch. The wick left behind is the evidence.

If the sweep aligns with my HTF bias (sweep low → long bias), the day is valid. If it goes against it, I stand down.

**Step 7 — wait for the breakout with displacement.**
Price must break the DR high (long) or DR low (short) with a real displacement candle:
- 1–2 candles, not a grind
- Large body, closing near the extreme
- It leaves a FVG behind

No displacement = no intent = no trade. **I never enter on the breakout candle.**

**Step 8 — set the alerts and walk away.**
This is the part that makes the strategy fit my schedule. Once the levels are drawn and the breakout is confirmed, I place alerts on the three entry levels and go do something else. The setup comes to me.

---

### The entry

**Step 9 — wait for the pullback into one of the three levels.**
In order of preference:

| Tier | Level | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | FVG / S&D zone inside the DR | Most precise, most used |
| 2 | DR high or DR low | Shallow retrace = strength |
| 3 | DR midpoint (50%) | Deep retrace, needs extra confirmation |

**Step 10 — confirm on the lower timeframe.**
The level gives me the area. The 1m/5m gives me the trigger. I need one of:
- A small MSS/CHoCH in my direction
- A clean rejection wick off the level
- Displacement away from the zone

**Never enter blind into a level.** The two conditions — right level + LTF defense — both have to be true.

**Step 11 — stop and targets, decided before entry.**
- **Stop:** below the invalidation (the sweep low for longs, the sweep high for shorts). Not at a comfortable distance. If the stop is too wide, I reduce size — I never move the stop.
- **T1:** nearest credible liquidity draw — opposite side of the DR, session high/low, nearest BSL/SSL. This is the main exit.
- **T2:** next external draw, only if structure clearly supports it.

**Step 12 — state check.**
Last question before clicking: **am I calm and following the model, or am I forcing this?**
If it's anything other than "following the model" — no trade. No exceptions.

---

### The whole thing in one sentence

> Wait for the HTF bias, let London sweep the liquidity, wait for the DR breakout with displacement, enter the pullback into the FVG/DR level with LTF confirmation, stop under the invalidation, target the next pool.

### Invalidations — when I kill the idea

- The breakout had no displacement (weak candle, immediate wick back inside)
- Price pushes straight through the pullback level with zero reaction
- The LTF never confirms after price reaches the zone
- Price re-enters the DR and starts accepting on the opposite side → **the trade is over**; holding it is hope, not trading

## Feedback loop of improvement

The strategy is fixed. I'm the variable.

This is the part most people skip, and it's the reason they stay at the same level for years. The technical knowledge is usually already there — the gap between where I am and where I want to be is almost entirely behavioural.

The whole loop rests on one distinction:

> A losing trade executed correctly is not a problem. A winning trade taken outside the model **is** a problem — because it reinforces a bad habit and pays me for it.

That's why I grade the process, not the money.

### After every trade — the journal entry

Every trade gets logged. Wins, losses and scratches. Also the trades I **didn't** take but should have, and the ones I took and shouldn't have.

If I only record entry and exit, the journal tells me nothing. It has to record the context, because the context is what I'm actually trying to improve.

| Instrument | |
| HTF bias | Bullish / bearish / neutral — **and why** |
| Dealing range | Premium / discount / at EQ |
| Liquidity event | Which pool was swept, and when |

| Daily profile | 01 London manip / 02 NY manip / 03 death / 04 trend |

### The process grade

The only score that matters.

- **A** — followed the model exactly. Win or lose, this trade was correct.
- **B** — minor deviation. Entered slightly early, moved a target, sized a bit off.
- **C** — major deviation. No narrative, no confirmation, wrong session, forced it.

Over a large sample, process grade and profitability converge. A month of A-grades with a red P&L is a strategy problem. A month of C-grades with a green P&L is a **worse** problem, because it's teaching me to keep doing it.

**The process is the product.**

### The four states — check before every entry

Before clicking, one question: *which state am I in right now?*

| State | What it looks like | Correct action |
|---|---|---|
| **Process** | Following the model exactly. Win or lose, the process is correct | This is the only state that gives full access to the edge. Proceed |
| **Foggy** | Uncertain about the setup but thinking about entering anyway. No clear narrative, no clear invalidation | Do not enter |
| **Revenge** | Triggered by a loss. Increasing size or taking unplanned trades to recover | Step away from the screen completely |
| **Flow** | Context stacks effortlessly, entries feel obvious. Product of good preparation | Protect it by *keeping* the routine — don't force extra trades |

If the honest answer is anything other than **process**, the trade doesn't get taken. This single habit removes more losses than any technical refinement.

### My triggers — the moments the model breaks

These are the specific situations that pull me out of process state. Knowing your own is not optional, it's infrastructure.

- **The first loss of the session.** Statistically the most dangerous trade of my day is the one right after it. Rule: after a loss, I close the charts for the session. London is over by then anyway.
- **Consecutive wins.** Paradoxically worse. Confidence becomes overconfidence, size creeps up, B and C setups start looking like A setups. "I'm in the zone" is not a valid reason to take a trade.
- **Missing a clean setup.** The FOMO trade. A missed trade is not a loss — chasing it usually is.
- **A big winner followed by a loss.** The "giving back profits" framing is destructive. The account doesn't care about the sequence.
- **High-impact news.** Conditions where the model may simply not apply. The pre-planned response is: no trade.
- **Being tired.** Specific to my schedule — 4:30 wake-ups on bad sleep. If I'm not sharp at 5:00, I mark the levels, set alerts and don't execute.

---

### The weekend recap

This is where the loop actually closes. Saturday or Sunday, one sitting.

**1 — Aggregate the process grades.**
How many A, B, C this week? The ratio is the real weekly result. The P&L is secondary.

**2 — Separate strategy losses from execution losses.**
Go through every loss and ask: was it an A-grade trade that just didn't work, or did I deviate? Only the second group is actionable. This distinction is the most valuable feedback available.

**3 — Find the pattern in the deviations.**
One bad trade is noise. The same deviation three times is a rule I'm missing. Examples: always entering before the DR window closes, always skipping the LTF confirmation on tier-1 entries, always trading after the first loss.

**4 — Check the narratives, not just the trades.**
Was my pre-session sentence right or wrong, and why? Was I reading the correct daily profile? This trains the read itself, separately from the execution.

**5 — Refine the checklist, not the strategy.**
Whatever I found in step 3 becomes a line in the pre-session checklist. This is the only thing that changes.

**6 — Plan the week ahead.**
Which days can I realistically be at the charts at 5:00? Which have meetings or high-impact news? Deciding in advance which days I'm *not* trading is part of the plan.

---

### What I'm allowed to change — and what I'm not

The loop only works if the strategy stays still. If I change the rules every time I lose, I never gather a sample, and without a sample there's nothing to learn from.

**Allowed to change:**
- The pre-session checklist (adding filters found in the recap)
- Risk per trade and position sizing
- Which days I trade
- How I journal
- Personal rules around my triggers (after a loss, tired, news)

**Not allowed to change:**
- The entry model (DR/IDR + S&D + the confluence hierarchy)
- The session (London ODR)
- The invalidation logic
- Anything, mid-week, because of one bad trade

A change to the model only gets considered after a full quarter of data, and only if the A-grade trades themselves are consistently failing. Anything else is emotion wearing the costume of an idea.

---

### The identity shift

The real point of all of this.

Stop being *"a trader who needs to win today"* and become *"a trader who executes a correct process consistently over hundreds of trades."*

When identity is attached to process instead of outcome, individual losses lose their emotional charge. I can review them clearly, take the lesson and move on without revenge.

The wrong question is "did I make money today?" — the answer is mostly noise.
The right question is "did I execute the model correctly today?" — that one always has a reliable answer, and it's the only one that compounds.

> The routine doesn't guarantee profits on any given day. It guarantees that over a large enough sample, the edge gets to express itself. Without it, edge expression is random. With it, it becomes systematic.
