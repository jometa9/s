## Giving Your Opinion — Taking a Position
 
## Introduction
 
This is step 4 of the plan I wrote in *"How I'm training myself to stop sounding basic in english meetings"*. The first three blocks were about surviving the meeting: **Lesson 1 — daily standup** (saying what I did), **Lesson 2 — asking for clarification** (making sure I understood), and **Lesson 3 — buying time** (not freezing when I don't have the answer yet).
 
Those three are defensive. They keep me from looking lost. But they don't make me a participant — I can do all three perfectly and still be the person who only reports status and never says what he thinks.
 
This block is where that changes. Now I have to **take a position**.
 
---
 
## Why This Matters
 
There's a moment in every meeting where someone asks *"what do you think?"* — and that question is not about English. It's about whether you're a developer who executes tickets or a developer whose opinion changes the decision.
 
As a non-native speaker there's a specific trap here. In Spanish I have a whole range: I can say something is a bad idea, or a bit risky, or fine but not my first choice. In English, without the phrases loaded, everything collapses into two options: **"yes, good"** or **silence**. So I default to silence, and the team learns my opinion doesn't exist.
 
**The cost of not having an opinion out loud:**
- ❌ Bad technical decisions get made because nobody objected
- ❌ You get treated as junior regardless of how much you know
- ❌ You end up implementing something you knew was wrong
- ❌ "He never says anything in meetings" shows up in your performance review
**The cost of saying it badly:** almost nothing. People forgive imperfect grammar. They do not forgive a developer who saw the problem and stayed quiet.
 
---
 
## The Fear Factor
 
What goes through my head before I speak:
- ❌ "What if I'm wrong in front of everyone?"
- ❌ "My English isn't good enough to argue this"
- ❌ "Maybe there's a reason they chose it and I'm missing it"
- ❌ "It's not my area, I shouldn't say anything"
**Reality:**
- ✅ An opinion with a reason is valuable **even when it's wrong** — it surfaces the trade-off
- ✅ You don't need perfect English to say "I'd go with the simpler one"
- ✅ "Maybe I'm missing something" is a **phrase**, not a reason to stay silent — say it and ask
- ✅ Being outside the area is often *why* your view is useful
---
 
## The Core Structure
 
Almost every opinion I need to give fits one shape. If I only memorize one thing from this block, it's this:
 
```
[Position] + [Reason] + [Opening]
 
I'd go with option B, because it's easier to roll back if it breaks. 
But I might be missing some context — what do you think?
```
 
- **Position** — what you actually think. First, not last.
- **Reason** — one reason, the strongest one. Not five.
- **Opening** — hands the floor back so it's a contribution, not a verdict.
 
The mistake I make is inverting it: three minutes of context, hedging, and then the opinion at the end, by which point someone already moved on. **Position first.**

## Situations & Examples
 
### Situation 1: Someone Asks You Directly
 
**Context:** "What do you think?" — the question I used to answer with "yeah, sounds good" regardless of what I thought.
 
**Bad way:**
```
Yeah, sounds good to me.
```
❌ Says nothing. This is what I say when I'm scared, and everyone can tell.
 
**Good way:**
```
I think it works, but my concern is the migration. If we change 
the schema now we have to backfill two million rows. Could we 
ship the endpoint first and migrate next sprint?
```
✅ Position + specific reason + a concrete alternative.
 
**Real example from work:**
 
Tech lead: "So we're rewriting the notifications service in Go. Thoughts?"
 
❌ Empty: "Sounds good."
 
✅ With a position: "I like the idea long term. My concern is that nobody on the team writes Go daily yet, so the first bugs will be slow to fix. Could we start with one non-critical service to build up experience?"
 
---
 
### Situation 2: You Have a Preference Between Two Options
 
**Context:** The team is choosing between two approaches and both are reasonable. You lean one way.
 
**Bad way:**
```
Both are fine, whatever you prefer.
```
❌ You had a preference and you threw it away. Now you're a spectator.
 
**Good way:**
```
I'd lean towards the second one. It's less code and we already 
use that pattern in the payments module, so it's one less thing 
to learn. Not a strong opinion though — if there's a reason to 
prefer the first one I'm happy either way.
```
✅ Clear lean + reason + honest about how strongly you hold it.
 
**Calibrating how strongly you hold it** is the underrated part:
```
Weak:    "I slightly prefer... but I don't feel strongly."
Medium:  "I'd go with X, because..."
Strong:  "I really think we should do X. Otherwise..."
```
Use the strong version rarely, so it means something when you do.
 
---
 
### Situation 3: You Disagree With a Technical Decision
 
**Context:** You think the decision is wrong. This is where the phrases matter most, because in English my instinct is either silence or something that sounds much harsher than I mean.
 
**Bad way (too harsh):**
```
No, that's wrong. That won't work.
```
❌ Correct or not, you just made it a fight.
 
**Bad way (too soft):**
```
Hmm, maybe, I don't know, it could be okay I guess...
```
❌ Nobody registers this as an objection. The decision passes.
 
**Good way:**
```
I see the appeal, but I'm worried about one thing: if the cache 
goes down, every request hits the database directly. Have we 
thought about what happens under load in that case?
```
✅ Acknowledges their side + names one specific risk + asks instead of declares.
 
**Real example from work:**
 
Manager: "Let's skip tests on this one, we need it out by Friday."
 
❌ Harsh: "That's a terrible idea."
 
✅ With a position: "I understand the deadline. My worry is this is the payment flow — if it breaks we find out from customers. Could we write tests just for the checkout path and skip the rest? That's maybe two extra hours."
 
---
 
### Situation 4: You're Asked About Something Outside Your Area
 
**Context:** Someone asks your opinion on infra, design, or product and it's not your specialty. Old me said "I don't know." That's a wasted turn.
 
**Bad way:**
```
I don't know, that's not really my area.
```
❌ True and useless.
 
**Good way:**
```
I don't have much context on the infra side, so take this lightly — 
but from the frontend it would help a lot if the response times 
were consistent, even if slower. Right now the variance is what 
breaks the UI.
```
✅ Flags the limits of your knowledge, then gives the view only you have.
 
---
 
### Situation 5: You Agree, But You Want to Add Something
 
**Context:** You basically agree. Just saying "I agree" adds nothing — agreement plus one detail makes you part of the decision.
 
**Bad way:**
```
Yeah, I agree.
```
❌ Fine, but forgettable.
 
**Good way:**
```
I agree with that. One thing I'd add: if we go that route we should 
also update the docs, because the current ones describe the old flow 
and new people will follow them.
```
✅ Agreement + something the room hadn't said yet.
 
---
 
### Situation 6: You Changed Your Mind
 
**Context:** You argued for something and now you think you were wrong. Saying so out loud is one of the strongest things you can do in a meeting.
 
**Bad way:**
```
[Silence, and quietly implement it the other way]
```
❌ Confusing for everyone who remembers the discussion.
 
**Good way:**
```
Actually, I want to walk back what I said earlier. After looking 
at the logs, the retry approach handles this better than my idea. 
Let's go with what Marta proposed.
```
✅ Explicit, credits the other person, and makes your future opinions more credible.
 
---
 
### Situation 7: You Genuinely Don't Have an Opinion
 
**Context:** Sometimes you really don't have one. Say that clearly rather than mumbling agreement.
 
**Bad way:**
```
Um... yeah... I guess... whatever you think...
```
❌ Reads as fear, not neutrality.
 
**Good way:**
```
I don't have a strong opinion on this one — I'd defer to whoever 
maintains that service. Both look reasonable to me.
```
✅ A deliberate position, and it protects the weight of your real opinions.

## Phrases for Giving Your Opinion
 
### Stating a Position
 
```
I think we should...
My take is...
I'd go with...
I'd lean towards...
From my perspective...
The way I see it...
If it were up to me, I'd...
```
 
### Softening a Strong Opinion
 
```
I might be missing some context, but...
Correct me if I'm wrong, but...
This is just my read on it...
I could be wrong here, but...
Take this with a grain of salt, but...
```
 
### Disagreeing Without Sounding Rude
 
```
I see it a bit differently.
I'm not sure I agree, and here's why...
That makes sense, but my concern is...
I hear you, though I'm worried about...
Have we considered what happens if...?
I like the idea, I'm just not sure about the timing.
```
 
### Agreeing and Adding
 
```
I agree, and I'd add that...
Exactly, and on top of that...
That's my read too. One thing worth flagging...
```
 
### Giving the Reason
 
```
...because it's simpler to roll back.
...because we already do that elsewhere.
...mainly for maintenance reasons.
The trade-off I see is...
```
 
### Handing the Floor Back
 
```
But what do you think?
Does that make sense?
Am I missing something?
Happy to be convinced otherwise.
Curious if anyone sees it differently.
```
 
### Changing Your Mind
 
```
Actually, I want to revise what I said.
Good point — that changes my view.
Fair enough, I'm convinced.
You're right, I hadn't considered that.
```

## Real-World Examples (Messy But Clear)
 
**Example 1 — Preference with a reason:**
```
I'd go with Postgres here. Mostly because everyone on the team 
already knows it, and this isn't the project where I want to be 
learning a new database at the same time.
```
 
**Example 2 — Disagreeing carefully:**
```
I hear you on the deadline. My concern is that this touches auth, 
and if we get it wrong the blast radius is every user. Could we 
push the smaller features instead?
```
 
**Example 3 — Opinion outside your area:**
```
I don't know much about the design system side, so take this 
lightly — but from the code, having three button variants that 
look almost identical causes a lot of confusion in review.
```
 
**Example 4 — Agreeing and adding:**
```
Yeah, that works for me. I'd just say we should do it early in 
the sprint, because if it breaks something we want the time to 
find out.
```
 
**Example 5 — No strong opinion:**
```
Honestly I don't have a strong preference on this one. Both work 
for what I need on my side, so I'd go with whatever is easier for 
the backend team.
```

## Key Rules for Giving Your Opinion
 
1. **Position first, context after**
   - ❌ Three minutes of background, then the opinion
   - ✅ "I'd go with B — because..."
2. **One reason, the strongest one**
   - ❌ Five reasons, which dilutes all of them
   - ✅ The single reason that would actually change someone's mind
3. **Disagree with the idea, never with the person**
   - ❌ "You didn't think this through."
   - ✅ "I'm worried this breaks under load."
4. **Ask instead of declare when you're unsure**
   - ✅ "Have we considered what happens if the cache is cold?"
   - Same objection, no confrontation, and it might have an answer
5. **Signal how strongly you hold it**
   - ✅ "Not a strong opinion" vs. "I really think we shouldn't do this"
   - Save the strong version for when it counts
6. **Don't apologize for having an opinion**
   - ❌ "Sorry, maybe this is stupid, but..."
   - ✅ "My take is..."
7. **Say it in the meeting, not in Slack afterwards**
   - ❌ Objecting privately to one person after the decision was made
   - ✅ Objecting while it's still a decision
8. **Have an opinion ready before the meeting**
   - If there's an agenda, pick your position in advance. The English gets much easier when you already know what you think.

## What NOT to Do
 
| ❌ Don't say | ✅ Say instead |
|---|---|
| "Yeah, sounds good." (when it doesn't) | "I think it works, but my concern is..." |
| "Both are fine, you decide." | "I'd lean towards B, because..." |
| "That's wrong." | "I see it differently — have we considered...?" |
| "Sorry, maybe this is stupid, but..." | "My take is..." |
| "I don't know, not my area." | "I don't have much context, but from my side..." |
| "I agree." (and nothing else) | "I agree, and I'd add that..." |
| *Staying silent, then complaining in Slack* | *Saying it while the decision is open* |
| "Um... I guess... whatever..." | "I don't have a strong opinion on this one." |

## The Bigger Picture
 
Giving your opinion is not:
- Arguing
- Being difficult
- Slowing down the decision
- Something you earn the right to do after N years
Giving your opinion IS:
- Part of the job you were hired for
- How technical decisions actually get stress-tested
- The difference between a ticket-executor and an engineer
- **A skill, not a personality trait**
The thing I keep having to remind myself: the goal isn't to be right. It's to put my view on the table so the team decides with it rather than without it. Being wrong out loud costs me almost nothing. Being silent costs the team the one perspective they didn't have.
 
And connecting back to the earlier blocks — this is where they pay off. **Lesson 2** lets me make sure I understood before I take a position on it, and **Lesson 3** buys me the three seconds I need to organize the position in English. Those two weren't the goal. They were the setup for this one.
 
Next block: **agreeing and disagreeing** — pushing back without sounding rude. Which is really this same skill, turned up.
